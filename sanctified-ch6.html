Chapter 6

Sanctified Through History and Understanding

Sanctification is not just personal—it is also historical. Bethesda Tabernacle recognized that many had carried the weight of misinterpreted Scripture for generations. Misunderstanding created fear, shame, and exclusion, particularly around identity and relational matters.

Pastor Joseph Bennett often taught: “To rewrite stories, we must understand history. Misreading Scripture leads to fear. Fear silences prayer. Fear distorts love. But truth restores.”

One evening, during a teaching session, Pastor Joseph began with 1 Corinthians 6:9-11. He explained the Greek terms often mistranslated or misused. The word malakoi, commonly rendered “effeminate,” historically referred to moral weakness, indulgence, and exploitation, not innate identity. Similarly, arsenokoitai referenced acts linked to pagan temple rituals and economic exploitation, not the natural capacity to love.

A young man named Daniel, who had grown up feeling condemned for his attraction to men, listened intently. Pastor Joseph assured the congregation: “Paul’s words were not addressing your heart’s capacity to love. They were confronting behaviors tied to abuse and exploitation, not God-given desire.”

During worship that night, Pastor Jason Barrett felt a strong impression from the Spirit. He walked among the congregation, praying over each member. Many began seeing visions. One woman saw herself in a temple of old Corinth. Shadows of ritualistic acts and oppression loomed. Suddenly, Jesus appeared, stepping into the shadows. Light radiated from Him, consuming the fear and replacing it with peace. “I see you,” Jesus whispered. “I know your heart. You are Mine.”

Pastor Joey Martin explained that understanding the historical context frees believers from unnecessary guilt. The world and culture have shifted. Ancient practices, coercion, and ritualized exploitation no longer define today’s believers. The Spirit of God meets us in present reality, offering restoration and affirmation.

Bethesda Tabernacle became a safe place where misinterpreted labels could be dismantled. Children and youth experienced this firsthand. One girl, Leah, saw visions of her ancestors being freed from chains of fear. Angels lifted them into light, and a voice whispered: “Your story is not bound by their shame. You are free in Me.”

Pastor Jason Morgan reminded the congregation: “History informs, but it does not define. God’s love transcends fear, labels, and misinterpretations. Your story is being rewritten.”

In another vision, Pastor Joseph Bennett saw a timeline of humanity stretching backward and forward. Each generation carried stories of condemnation, fear, and misunderstanding. As he watched, Jesus walked along the timeline, touching each generation, and the chains dissolved. Light replaced the shadows. Angels lifted the rewritten stories, presenting them before the throne.

Members began understanding that restoration is not conditional on identity or labels but on surrender to Jesus. Pastor Jason Barrett emphasized: “Sanctification is not separation from the world. It is alignment with Me. Your calling is not determined by what men say about you but by My presence within you.”

During an evening prayer meeting, Pastor Joey Martin and several members experienced a supernatural encounter together. The sanctuary transformed into a river of light. Members stepped into the river, representing baptism into Christ’s Spirit. Chains of guilt, shame, and fear dissolved. Angels guided them through, each step affirming belonging, identity, and sanctification.

Bethesda Tabernacle’s teaching on historical and biblical context became a cornerstone for the congregation. They began to understand that love itself is never sin. Misinterpreted acts in history do not condemn God-given capacity for love today. The Spirit of God confirms identity, purpose, and belonging in each life.

Pastor Joseph Bennett closed the session with a prophetic word: “Do not fear what the world has labeled you. Do not fear what the past has imposed on you. Step into the present reality. Jesus rewrites all stories. Your past, your labels, your history—none of it can stand against My Spirit. You are sanctified, beloved, restored.”

By the end of the night, members felt liberation. They understood that sanctification is not only personal but also historical—it is the reclamation of truth, restoration of identity, and rewriting of legacy in Christ. Bethesda Tabernacle became more than a church—it became a sanctuary of understanding, freedom, and supernatural confirmation.
