Chapter 4

Sanctified Beyond the Power of Labels

Bethesda Tabernacle quickly realized that labels carry immense weight. Many had arrived burdened by words from parents, society, schools, or churches—words like “unworthy,” “sinner,” “too broken,” “different,” or “unlovable.” These labels shaped identity long before anyone encountered the love of God.

Pastor Joseph Bennett, Teacher and Pastor, often reminded the congregation: “Labels describe experience, not destiny. God renames people to reveal identity. Abram became Abraham. Jacob became Israel. Simon became Peter. Labels do not define calling. God does.”

One evening, Pastor Jason Barrett began a service with a simple, yet profound question: “Who has ever been labeled by the world? Who has carried a name that made you feel less than what God created you to be?” Hands rose across the sanctuary. Members shared stories of childhood shame, church judgment, and societal rejection. Some wept openly. Others trembled quietly. The weight of labels was visible in every posture.

Pastor Joey Martin spoke directly to the heart of the matter: “Sanctification is not about removing stories. It is about rewriting them in Christ. Your label does not define your destiny. Your identity is found in Me. I am Jesus, and you are Mine.”

During worship that night, multiple members experienced visions. One woman saw herself as a small child trapped in a dark room labeled “unworthy.” The shadows of past judgment loomed over her. Suddenly, beams of golden light pierced the darkness. Jesus appeared, kneeling beside her, gently brushing away the words scrawled across the walls. “I see you. You are Mine. Step into My light.”

A young man named Elijah saw himself walking through a forest, each tree marked with a name society had given him: “Failed,” “Rejected,” “Shameful.” As he walked, angels appeared along the path, holding torches. Each torch lit a tree on fire, not to destroy, but to consume the lies and reveal the truth beneath. Jesus stood at the end of the path, arms open, saying: “Your story is Mine to rewrite. Come forward.”

Pastor Jason Morgan reminded the congregation that Scripture consistently speaks to restoration and belonging. Romans 10:13 says: “Whoever calls on the name of the Lord shall be saved.” Salvation and acceptance are not determined by labels, appearances, or past mistakes—they are determined by relationship with Jesus.

Bethesda intentionally created an environment where labels could not limit identity. Children ran freely in the sanctuary, their laughter echoing through the space. Adults prayed openly, sharing testimonies of how they had been silenced elsewhere. Every story became part of the larger narrative of redemption.

During extended prayer, Pastor Joseph Bennett shared historical context about the fear-driven misinterpretation of Scripture. 1 Corinthians 6:9-11 had often been weaponized to shame same-sex desire or other differences. He explained: Paul was addressing behaviors tied to exploitation, idolatry, and ritualistic abuse, not the intrinsic identity or God-given love of individuals. Then he read aloud 1 Corinthians 6:11: “And such were some of you. But you were washed, sanctified, justified in the name of the Lord Jesus Christ and by the Spirit of our God.” The congregation heard clearly that restoration was available for all.

As Pastor Jason Barrett prayed over the congregation, beams of light descended from the ceiling. Members described feeling warmth, invisible hands lifting burdens, and an overwhelming sense of acceptance. Jesus was touching their stories, confirming that labels do not limit His power to redeem.

One woman, Clara, who had been labeled as “too broken for church,” experienced a vision of angels removing her chains. She saw a new name written above her head in light: Beloved, Chosen, Restored. She felt the Spirit whisper: “I have not abandoned you. You are Mine. Walk forward.”

The teaching that night was clear: sanctification means belonging before behaving. Members were encouraged to embrace their identity in Christ, to step into calling without fear, and to reject the labels the world or past had placed on them. Pastor Joey Martin reminded the congregation: “Your purpose flows from presence, not perfection. You cannot step into calling if fear dictates your story.”

By the end of the night, members began to understand the power of rewritten stories. Sanctification is not merely internal—it is lived, experienced, and confirmed. Members saw supernatural confirmation: visions of light, angels, broken chains, and Jesus embracing them personally. The sanctuary became a living proof that labels cannot limit God’s power or calling.

Bethesda Tabernacle’s mission became unmistakable: every person entering this house is welcomed as they are, affirmed in Christ, and invited to step into the life God has always intended.

The chapter closes with Pastor Jason Barrett’s reflection: “Sanctified is not a building or a name. It is a posture. It is Jesus standing where we are, rewriting our stories. Every label is broken, every chain dissolved, every soul known. This is the house where healing meets purpose, and where fear has no power.”
