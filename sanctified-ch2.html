Chapter 2

Sanctified Is Christ Formed Within

Bethesda Tabernacle discovered early that a room full of people does not guarantee hearts full of God. Many churches focus on numbers, performances, or outward appearances. Bethesda focused on presence—the presence of Jesus dwelling inside every life. Pastor Jason Barrett often preached from Colossians 1:27: “Christ in you, the hope of glory.”

This truth became foundational: sanctification is not about external perfection; it is about internal transformation. It is Christ being formed within a person, reshaping thoughts, healing wounds, and restoring identity from the inside out.

One Sunday morning, a man named Elijah entered Bethesda quietly. He sat in the back, avoiding eye contact. He had spent years trying to measure up to expectations he could never meet. Every church he attended left him feeling inadequate. During worship, he closed his eyes and whispered, “Jesus, I don’t know how to be what they expect.”

In that moment, Elijah felt a warmth rise within his chest. It was not overwhelming, but steady and undeniable. He later described it as a light growing inside him. He saw, in his spirit, Jesus standing before him—not distant, not disappointed, but present and compassionate. Jesus spoke softly: “I am not asking you to become someone else. I am forming Myself within you.”

Elijah wept quietly. For the first time, he understood that sanctification was not about striving; it was about surrender. It was about allowing Jesus to live within him, guiding every step.

Pastor Joseph Bennett expanded on this during teaching sessions. He explained that sanctification is often misunderstood as behavior modification. In reality, it is transformation at the core of a person’s being. “When Christ is formed within you,” he said, “your actions follow naturally. You do not force change; change flows from presence.”

Another member, Rachel, experienced this truth during a midweek prayer service. She had struggled with anxiety for years. Her mind raced constantly, filled with fear and doubt. As the congregation prayed, she felt a sudden stillness. It was as if the noise in her mind quieted instantly. She described it as a gentle hand calming her thoughts.

In that stillness, she heard a whisper: “My peace is within you.” Rachel realized that she had been searching for peace externally, when Jesus had already placed it within her. Sanctification, for her, became the process of learning to live from that inner peace rather than external chaos.

Pastor Jason Morgan often reminded the church that sanctification is deeply personal. “Jesus meets each person where they are,” he would say. “He does not use a single mold. He forms Himself uniquely within each life.”

This understanding changed how the church approached growth. Instead of pressuring people to conform to a specific image, Bethesda encouraged individuals to seek Jesus personally. Growth was measured not by outward appearance but by inward transformation.

During one service, a young man named Caleb shared his testimony. He had spent years trying to suppress parts of himself to fit into religious expectations. At Bethesda, he encountered a different message: Jesus wanted to transform him, not erase him. Caleb described feeling as though Jesus was rebuilding him piece by piece, restoring what had been broken rather than discarding it.

“I thought I had to become someone else,” Caleb said. “But Jesus is showing me who I was always meant to be.”

Sanctification at Bethesda became a journey of discovery. People began to see that Christ within them was not distant or abstract. He was active, present, and personal. He spoke, guided, and restored.

Even in moments of struggle, this truth remained. Members were taught that failure does not disqualify them. Instead, it becomes an opportunity to return to the presence of Jesus within them. Sanctification is not a straight path; it is a relationship.

Pastor Joey Martin emphasized purpose within this transformation. “When Christ is formed within you,” he taught, “you begin to understand your calling. You begin to see why you were created. Sanctification is not just about healing; it is about stepping into purpose.”

As weeks turned into months, the congregation witnessed profound changes. People who once felt lost began to walk with confidence. Those who carried shame began to speak with clarity. The transformation was not forced; it was natural, flowing from the presence of Jesus within them.

Bethesda Tabernacle continued to reinforce this message: sanctification is Christ formed within. It is the process of becoming who Jesus created you to be, not through effort, but through relationship.

And as each person allowed Christ to dwell within them, the house itself became a living testimony. Sanctification was no longer an abstract concept. It was visible, tangible, and alive in every transformed life.
