Chapter 2

Sanctified Is Christ Formed Within

Bethesda Tabernacle discovered early that a room full of people does not guarantee hearts full of God. Many churches focus on numbers, performances, or outward appearances. Bethesda focused on presence—the presence of Jesus dwelling inside every life. Pastor Jason Barrett often preached from Colossians 1:27: “Christ in you, the hope of glory.”

The congregation quickly learned that sanctification is not about compliance with rules or behavior. It is about Christ formed within. Transformation begins on the inside. External compliance without internal habitation produces temporary change, but when Jesus dwells fully in a heart, change becomes inevitable.

Pastor Jason often told the story of Zacchaeus. “Jesus did not demand transformation before fellowship,” he preached one Sunday. “Zacchaeus was still a tax collector, still a sinner in the eyes of society, but Jesus saw the heart. Transformation came through His presence, not through public approval.”

One night during extended prayer, Pastor Joey Martin experienced a vivid vision. He saw members entering the sanctuary carrying chains of shame, fear, and guilt. Some had been labeled as unworthy, others had been shamed for their identity, and some simply carried invisible burdens from childhood. Jesus walked among them, touching the chains. As He did, the chains dissolved. Pastor Joey felt warmth flood the congregation as if invisible hands were lifting their burdens.

During that same prayer session, Pastor Joseph Bennett felt the Spirit guide him to each person. He whispered encouragement, explained Scripture, and reminded each member of the promises of God. Romans 8:10 echoed in his teaching: “If Christ is in you, the Spirit is life because of righteousness.” He reminded the congregation that internal transformation is the mark of sanctification, not outward performance.

Bethesda became a sanctuary for those who had been silenced. Members who had once hidden in shame began speaking and praying openly. A young man named Eli had carried years of guilt over his past mistakes. During a vision, he saw himself standing at the foot of a mountain, chains dangling from his wrists. Jesus appeared beside him, reaching out His hand. “Step forward,” Jesus whispered. Eli took a trembling step, and the chains shattered. That moment marked a turning point for him. He realized that being filled with Christ does not require perfection—it requires surrender.

Pastor Jason Barrett often reminded the congregation: “Sanctification is alignment with Christ’s presence, not isolation from the world. You cannot separate yourself from humanity to be holy. You become holy by allowing Jesus to live inside you and to work through you.”

In the following weeks, supernatural experiences became a normal part of the church’s life. During worship, some members reported seeing streams of golden light descending onto them. Others felt warmth enveloping their bodies. Pastor Jason Morgan described one vision where the sanctuary walls seemed to dissolve, and the congregation found themselves surrounded by the light of heaven. Angels floated among them, their presence gentle yet powerful, confirming the presence of God in the midst of the worshippers.

Children also experienced supernatural affirmation. Little ones who had never felt safe in a church began running freely in the sanctuary, laughing and singing. Some spoke spontaneously, recounting visions of angels or hearing Jesus’ voice telling them they were loved and known. Pastor Joey Martin would kneel to meet them, reminding the congregation: “Children do not need performance to be accepted. They only need presence.”

Bethesda’s teaching emphasized that sanctification is not a set of rules to follow. It is a process of Christ forming within each believer. Pastor Joseph Bennett often shared the example of the early church in Acts 2. The believers were filled with the Holy Ghost, not after achieving perfection, but through surrender and faith. The Spirit of God changed them from the inside, producing lives of purpose, power, and joy.

One night, during a particularly powerful prayer meeting, multiple members reported experiencing simultaneous visions. A woman saw herself in a garden where angels were planting seeds in the soil around her. Each seed represented a gift, a calling, or a promise of God. A young man saw himself walking across a bridge surrounded by darkness, but Jesus appeared as the light guiding him across. Every vision confirmed the same truth: sanctification begins where Christ dwells within, and His presence produces life, healing, and purpose.

Pastor Jason Barrett reminded the congregation that sanctification is not static. It is dynamic. “Christ does not dwell in a corner of your heart,” he said, “He dwells fully. He reshapes, restores, and rewrites every part of your story.”

By the end of the chapter’s narrative arc, members had begun to understand that their identity is not determined by labels, past mistakes, or human judgment. Identity is revealed in relationship with Jesus. Sanctification is internal habitation by the Spirit of God, which naturally produces external alignment in love, purpose, and holiness.

Bethesda Tabernacle became known as a place where supernatural experiences were natural, where visions and angelic encounters confirmed God’s promises, and where sanctification was measured not by attendance, but by the depth of Christ formed within each believer.

The chapter concludes with Pastor Jason Morgan’s vision of the sanctuary filled with light. Each member stood bathed in a golden glow. The Lord spoke: “I dwell in you. I am rewriting your story. Do not fear the past. Step into My presence, and you will be made whole.”

Sanctified begins not in behavior, performance, or perfection. It begins in Christ dwelling within. Bethesda Tabernacle became the living example of that truth.
