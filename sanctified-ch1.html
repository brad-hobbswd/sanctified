Chapter 1

Sanctified Begins Where Jesus Stands

Sanctified does not begin where people expect it to begin. It does not begin with perfection, rules, or moral achievement. It begins where Jesus stands, waiting, knowing every wound, seeing every scar, and inviting every heart. Bethesda Tabernacle was born from this truth.

Pastor Jason Barrett, Founder and Lead Pastor, had carried a burden for years. He watched people leave churches broken, discouraged, or ashamed. He saw believers fluent in Scripture yet unfamiliar with rest. He saw people worshiping privately but hiding publicly. He watched children enter Sunday school full of curiosity but leave carrying labels that silenced their joy. Pastor Jason knew that churches could appear full yet leave souls empty.

One night, in extended prayer and fasting, Pastor Jason was taken into a vision that would define the house forever. He saw a pool, still and crowded. It resembled the pool of Bethesda described in John 5. People had been waiting there for years, some for decades. Some limped. Some stood silently, afraid to approach. Jesus stood beside the water, not judging, not counting, simply watching. Angels descended, not to separate, but to stir the water. Where it had been stagnant, it now moved. A song of heaven seemed to echo around him: “Do not be afraid. The One who made you has not left you. He sees you. He knows you. Step forward.”

Pastor Jason felt the weight of the vision settle over him. The Lord spoke directly: “This house will not measure success by numbers. It will not measure success by appearances. It will measure success by the restoration of My children. Bring them to Me, and My Spirit will do the rest.”

Days later, Pastor Jason Morgan, Cofounder and Associate Pastor, received a dream that confirmed the vision. He saw doors swinging open across the city. People hesitated at each threshold, unsure if they were welcome. At every door, Jesus appeared, arms open wide, saying: “Come in. I already know your name. I have been waiting for you.” The dream was vivid. Pastor Jason Morgan felt the wind of heaven brush his face as if angels were actually guiding him through each door.

Pastor Joey Martin, Purpose Pastor, received a prophetic word in prayer: “This house will focus on purpose, not perfection. This house will call My children into calling, not condemnation. This house will not ask who they were, but will ask who they can become in Me.”

Pastor Joseph Bennett, Teacher and Pastor, received his own confirmation. In a vision, he saw scrolls opening in the Spirit. Letters lifted off the scrolls like ethereal smoke, revealing long-misunderstood passages of Scripture. The Spirit whispered: “Do not fear the words of men. I am rewriting what has been misunderstood. These words are for life, not judgment. Teach them rightly.”

From that night, Bethesda Tabernacle began its journey. Sanctified was not a title; it was a posture. It was an invitation to step into healing, identity, and purpose before performance. It was a declaration that fear and shame had no place where Jesus dwells.

Weeks later, the congregation began to witness the supernatural manifest in subtle but undeniable ways. One young woman, Mia, came to the church carrying the weight of years of judgment from her family. During worship, she suddenly felt invisible hands lift from her shoulders. She described seeing chains fall away as if unseen angels were literally releasing them. She wept for the first time in years, feeling a freedom she had never known.

Another member, Thomas, who had never felt safe in a church, saw a vision of light surrounding him in the sanctuary. The light pulsed in rhythm with his heartbeat. Jesus appeared and whispered, “I have never left you. You are Mine.” That moment marked the beginning of Thomas’ understanding of belonging: sanctification begins with being seen and embraced by Jesus, not by conformity to human expectations.

Pastor Jason Barrett often taught the congregation that sanctification begins with the presence of Jesus. External performance is temporary. True restoration flows from the Spirit. “Come to Me,” he said during a Sunday morning service, “not because you are worthy, but because you are Mine. I do not require perfection. I require presence. I do not demand compliance. I demand surrender.”

Angels continued to appear in visions to members during prayer meetings. One night, a vision revealed angels lining the walls of the sanctuary, each holding a candle of hope. Pastor Jason Morgan described it as a river of light flowing into the pews. The angels were not guarding the doors; they were welcoming souls into restoration.

Bethesda Tabernacle became known as a place where labels, fear, and shame could not dictate a person’s value. Pastor Joey Martin often reminded members: “Do not let the world name you. Do not let the past define you. Let Jesus define you. Sanctified means belonging before behaving.”

Even the children felt the atmosphere. Little ones who had never experienced the love of a safe church ran freely in the sanctuary, laughing, singing, and interacting with pastors who knelt to meet them at eye level. Each interaction became a lesson in love, affirmation, and restoration.

Pastor Joseph Bennett often reflected on the vision of the scrolls. He taught that the Word of God is alive and active, but misinterpretation can create fear where there should be freedom. Bethesda committed to reading Scripture correctly, explaining context, and teaching that God’s heart is always for restoration, not condemnation.

The first months of Bethesda were marked by supernatural affirmation. Members reported feeling a tangible presence during worship. Some saw visions of their younger selves being embraced by Jesus. Others felt warmth or light surrounding them as they prayed. Every vision, every encounter, reinforced the same truth: this house was built to rewrite stories through Jesus.

Sanctified is not a label for the house. It is a posture of surrender, a recognition that healing begins where Jesus stands. Bethesda Tabernacle was a place where people could bring every wound, every doubt, every fear, and step into restoration. It was a place where Jesus rewrites stories daily.

And in those early days, the pastors realized that every encounter—angelic, prophetic, or natural—was a confirmation: this house would not rely on seats or numbers, but on lives transformed, hearts restored, and souls filled with Jesus.

Sanctified had begun. And from the first vision at the pool to the first tear shed in worship, it was clear: Bethesda Tabernacle was a sanctuary where Jesus’ love rewrites every story, one heart at a time.
